The last Twilight film came out in 2012. By 2013, the franchise had become the internet's favourite punching bag — the blue filter, the sparkling, the "spider monkey." By 2015, making fun of Twilight was practically a personality. And then something strange happened.

People started watching it again. Not ironically. Not to mock it. Because they genuinely wanted to.

The Twilight Renaissance is real, it is documented, and it is still growing. Here's why it happened — and why it is not going away.

When Did the Renaissance Start?

The turning point was autumn 2020. The factors that converged were almost comically perfect. The world was locked down. Netflix had just added the Twilight films to its catalogue in several major markets. Stephenie Meyer released Midnight Sun — the long-awaited companion novel told from Edward's perspective — in August of that year. BookTok was exploding with paranormal romance recommendations. And people had nothing to do but rewatch things they remembered from their childhoods.

Google Trends data showed searches for "Bella Swan" spiking in July 2021 to their highest level since 2013 — the year after Breaking Dawn Part 2 came out. They have remained consistently elevated ever since. The franchise did not just experience a nostalgic blip. It found a second audience.

By 2022, #twilight had accumulated billions of views on TikTok. The "Dazzling Look" filter — which gives users the pale, slightly luminescent skin of a Cullen — had been used over 100,000 times. Songs from the soundtrack, particularly Bon Iver's Roslyn and Muse's Supermassive Black Hole, were being used in hundreds of thousands of videos. The renaissance had its own aesthetic, its own soundtrack, and its own language.

Six Reasons the Renaissance Happened

01 — TikTok's algorithm rewards sincere emotional engagement

TikTok's algorithm does not distinguish between irony and sincerity. A video about Twilight that makes people feel something — whether that's nostalgia, humour, or genuine love for the characters — performs the same way. This gave fans permission to be openly enthusiastic in a way that earlier social media cultures had actively punished. The pile-on culture of Twitter circa 2012 that had made loving Twilight embarrassing did not translate to TikTok, where earnestness and emotional authenticity are rewarded rather than mocked.

02 — Gen Z discovered it without the cultural baggage

The generation that is driving the Twilight Renaissance was too young to experience the original cultural moment and, crucially, too young to experience the backlash that followed. They discovered the films on streaming services and encountered them as what they actually are: strange, sincere, atmospheric pieces of early 2000s cinema. Without the context of "you had to be embarrassed to like this," they could engage with it on its own terms. The mocking that had shaped the 2010s conversation simply did not apply to them.

03 — The "recession indicator" theory

Fans and cultural commentators began noticing that peaks in Twilight engagement seemed to correlate with periods of economic anxiety. The original Twilight phenomenon ran from 2008 to 2012 — almost exactly the years of the global financial crisis. The Renaissance began in 2020 with the pandemic. The theory, shared widely on social media, is that Twilight functions as comfort media during times of collective stress: familiar, emotionally intense, set in a world where the biggest problem is a love triangle. Fans have explicitly described rewatching the films as a way of returning to a time when the world felt more manageable. As one widely shared Reddit comment put it: "I always bring out the Twilight movies if I'm stressed with adult life and wanna feel like a kid again."

04 — Feminist reassessment changed the conversation

The decade-long critical conversation about Twilight — much of which focused on Bella's passivity and the troubling power dynamics of the Edward relationship — gradually gave way to a more nuanced reading. Younger critics began pointing out that dismissing the things teenage girls love as inherently inferior is itself a form of cultural bias. The intense scrutiny applied to Twilight was not applied to, say, the Fast and Furious franchise, despite the latter's arguably more questionable gender politics. Re-evaluating Twilight through this lens allowed fans to love it without feeling defensive about its flaws.

05 — Twilightcore became a genuine aesthetic movement

The visual language of the first Twilight film — the blue-green desaturated colour grade, the rain-soaked Pacific Northwest forests, Bella's wardrobe of earth tones and flannels and Converse — became the template for a full aesthetic movement online. Twilightcore, also called Bella Swan Core, is a documented aesthetic category on every major platform. It encompasses fashion, photography filters, interior design choices, and mood boards. The aesthetic works independently of the films — someone who has never watched Twilight can engage with Twilightcore. This created a pipeline: aesthetic first, then curiosity about where it came from, then watching the films.

06 — The memes became affectionate

The Twilight meme ecosystem shifted from mockery to affection somewhere around 2021. The same scenes that had been endlessly ridiculed — the baseball scene, Jasper's pained expressions, the spider monkey line — began to be celebrated rather than attacked. This is a subtle but significant distinction: the humour was no longer "look how bad this is" but "look how wonderfully itself this is." Jasper's constipated expression during the birthday party scene became a beloved TikTok format. The baseball scene was recontextualised as genuinely iconic. The shift in how the memes functioned signalled a shift in how the culture related to the franchise.

The Jasper Effect — One Character's Unexpected Virality

Of all the Twilight Renaissance's unexpected developments, none has been more surprising than Jasper Hale's emergence as perhaps the most consistently viral character on TikTok. Jasper — the empath who spent decades as a human-feeding vampire before joining the Cullens and who struggles most visibly with the vegetarian lifestyle — spends most of the saga looking like he is in intense physical discomfort. Jackson Rathbone's performance, which was presumably meant to convey supernatural restraint, translates to modern audiences as a man suffering magnificently at all times.

The Jasper meme format has generated hundreds of millions of views. Fans edit him into situations where his expression is contextually appropriate — a dentist's waiting room, a difficult work meeting, a first date going badly. The memes have become a love language. Rathbone has leaned into them with graceful self-awareness, and the result has made Jasper more culturally present in 2026 than he was at the height of the original franchise.

BookTok and the Paranormal Romance Revival

The Twilight Renaissance did not happen in isolation. It was part of a broader revival of paranormal romance as a genre, driven largely by BookTok — the TikTok community dedicated to books and reading. The pandemic years saw an enormous spike in reading, and BookTok channelled that energy into specific recommendations. Paranormal romance — vampires, werewolves, fae, magic — was one of the biggest beneficiaries.

Twilight, as the defining text of the genre for an entire generation, became the natural reference point. Creators recommending books like Vampire Academy, A Court of Thorns and Roses, or the Dark Olumpus series would often reference Twilight as context. The franchise became a cultural anchor — a shared text that creators and audiences could use to shorthand an entire emotional register. "If you liked the feeling of Twilight, read this" became a standard recommendation format on BookTok, and it consistently drove new readers back to Meyer's original series.

The Netflix Series and What Comes Next

The Twilight Renaissance did not go unnoticed by the entertainment industry. In 2023, Lionsgate Television announced a new Twilight adaptation was in development. By September 2024, it had been confirmed as an animated series for Netflix, adapting Midnight Sun — the 2020 novel that retells Twilight from Edward's perspective. Stephenie Meyer is attached as executive producer. Sinead Daly (Tell Me Lies, The Walking Dead: World Beyond) is writing.

The choice to adapt Midnight Sun rather than simply remake Twilight is interesting strategically. It allows the new series to exist in dialogue with the original films rather than replacing them — viewers who love the 2008 movie can watch the animated series as a companion piece. It also means the animated series begins from Edward's perspective, which is exactly what the TikTok generation has been wanting since Midnight Sun was published. The most common lament in the Twilight Renaissance community is that Edward's inner life — his century of accumulated loneliness, his terror at his own feelings — was not visible in the films. An animated series built around that perspective is a direct response to what the renaissance audience has been asking for.

Why It Matters Beyond Nostalgia

The easy explanation for the Twilight Renaissance is nostalgia — people rewatching the films of their childhoods because the present is hard. And nostalgia is certainly part of it. But it does not fully explain why Twilight, specifically, rather than the dozens of other franchises from the same era. It does not explain why people who were not alive for the original moment are joining the conversation. And it does not explain why the engagement has deepened rather than faded over time.

The more honest answer is that Twilight contains something genuinely unusual: a mainstream story in which the protagonist's primary desire is transformation rather than belonging. Bella does not want to fit in. She wants to become something different from what she is. That desire — coded in supernatural terms, set against the most atmospheric rain-soaked backdrop imaginable — resonates with people who feel like they were born for something the world around them cannot see. That is not a feeling that goes away with time. If anything, it intensifies in adulthood. The Twilight Renaissance is, at its core, a lot of people recognising that the feeling they had at thirteen was true.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Twilight Renaissance? The Twilight Renaissance refers to the significant cultural reassessment of the Twilight Saga that began around 2020–2021, primarily driven by Gen Z discovering the films on streaming platforms and TikTok content going viral. What had been treated as a cultural punchline in the 2010s was re-evaluated as a distinctive, sincere, and genuinely atmospheric piece of early 2000s pop culture. The Renaissance is characterised by a shift from ironic mockery to genuine affection.

Why is Twilight trending on TikTok? The #twilight hashtag on TikTok has accumulated over 10 billion views, driven by a combination of nostalgic content from original fans, new viewers discovering the films for the first time, the Twilightcore aesthetic trend, meme formats built around specific scenes and characters (particularly Jasper), and clips from the films that read as unintentionally funny or charming to modern audiences.

Is the Twilight Renaissance still happening in 2026? Yes — the Renaissance shows no signs of fading. The announcement of the Netflix animated series has added a new wave of interest. TikTok engagement with Twilight content remains consistently high. Meyer's books continue to sell strongly globally. And the aesthetic and cultural language of Twilight has become so embedded in online culture that it functions as a reference point for the broader paranormal romance genre.

What is the "recession indicator" theory about Twilight? The theory, widely shared on social media, suggests that peaks in Twilight's cultural popularity correlate with periods of economic anxiety — the original 2008–2012 run coincided with the global financial crisis, and the 2020–present Renaissance began with the pandemic. The theory posits that Twilight functions as comfort media during collective stress, offering emotional intensity, familiar characters, and a world where the biggest problems are supernatural and solvable.

Why do people love Twilight again after hating it? Several factors contributed: a generational shift that removed the cultural stigma of liking it, a feminist reassessment of why "things teenage girls love" were disproportionately mocked, TikTok's algorithm rewarding sincere engagement over ironic distance, and a genuine re-evaluation of the films as distinctive pieces of early 2000s cinema that have aged more interestingly than many of their contemporaries.
