The Twilight Saga soundtracks are remarkable cultural artifacts — curated collections that defined a generation's relationship with indie, alternative, and atmospheric music. The first film's soundtrack in particular became one of the best-selling soundtrack albums of the 2000s, launching careers and reintroducing artists to entirely new audiences. Here is the complete guide to every song across all five films and scores.

The original Twilight (2008) soundtrack sold over 3 million copies in the United States alone, certified 3× Platinum by the RIAA. It remains one of the defining alternative soundtrack albums of the 2000s.

Twilight (2008) — The Iconic Original Soundtrack

Alexandra Patsavas — whose credits include The O.C., Grey's Anatomy, and Gossip Girl — curated a soundtrack of primarily indie and alternative artists that became the sonic identity of the entire saga. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most famous song from Twilight?

Decode by Paramore and Flightless Bird, American Mouth by Iron & Wine are the two most enduring songs from the original Twilight (2008). From the entire saga, A Thousand Years by Christina Perri (Breaking Dawn Part 1) became the most commercially successful, still widely used at weddings today.

Who composed the Twilight score?

The Twilight film scores were composed by three different composers: Carter Burwell (Twilight, 2008; Breaking Dawn Parts 1 & 2), Alexandre Desplat (New Moon, 2009), and Howard Shore (Eclipse, 2010).

Does Robert Pattinson sing in Twilight?

Yes — Robert Pattinson wrote and performed two original songs for the Twilight (2008) soundtrack: Never Think and Let Me Sign. Both are genuinely good pieces of indie folk music, not novelty tracks.

What song plays at the Twilight prom?

Flightless Bird, American Mouth by Iron & Wine plays during the prom scene in Twilight (2008) — one of the most emotionally effective uses of a song in the entire saga. The same song is reprised at Bella and Edward's wedding in Breaking Dawn Part 1.

What band wrote Supermassive Black Hole for Twilight?

Supermassive Black Hole is by Muse — it was not written specifically for Twilight but was licensed from their 2006 album Black Holes and Revelations. Its use in the baseball scene became one of the most iconic soundtrack-to-scene pairings in the entire saga. Muse later wrote Neutron Star Collision specifically for Eclipse (2010).
